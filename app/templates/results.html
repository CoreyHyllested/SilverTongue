Confidence

Filler word rate

This metric measures how often you say any of the following: 'so', 'like', 'um', 'okay', and 'just'. Sure, there are legitimate uses for all those words, and in TED talks there's an average of around one of them every minute. But in general these words are used to fill in gaps when the speaker doesn't know what they want to say next.

Similar to using filler words, it's easy to get tripped up in the middle of a statement not knowing where you're headed and repeat a word while you sort out your thoughts. TED speakers have this habit pretty well eradicated.

Pacing

Average words per minute

{% if not ready %}
{% else %}
{% endif %}
Finding the optimal speaking speed is a balance between clarity, energy, and information transfer. Speak too quickly and people start to lose you. Speak too slowly and people start to get bored. TED speakers tend to speak at about 150 words per minute, but there's a wide range of speaking rates represented in TED talk, so don't worry if you're a bit off that mark.

Speed variation

{% if not ready %}
{% else %}
{% endif %}
Barreling through your talk at a constant speed risks leaving the audience shell-shocked. To keep your audience engaged, you want to speak more quickly at some point to raise the energy level, but slow down to emphasize an important point. TED speakers tend to vary their speed by about 20% on average.

Pitch variation

A monotone presentation can be painful to watch, but a wildly excited speaker can be equally off-putting. The sweet spot in pitch variation for TED speakers is around 25%.

Clarity

Average word clarity score

{% if not ready %}
{% else %}
{% endif %}
This metric, powered by SpeechAce, listens to the individual sounds of each word the speaker says and compares them to the accepted pronunciation of that word. Consequently, this score will also be reduced when the speaker says 'um' or something similar just before a word. Even TED speakers only reach a 90% clarity score on average, so don't worry about getting your number up to 100%. That would probably require you to speak much more slowly than is optimal. Find a balance that works for you!
